Fitting Easy

A mobile application that improves the in-store clothing shopping experience for wheelchair users

Team: Jemma Yang, Karthik Srinivasan, Kaylin Broussard, Yujia Wang

Duration: Aug 2017 - Dec 2017

Key Words: Universal Design, Clothing Shopping, Mobile Application

My Role: UX Design, UI Design, Usability Testing

Overview

Problem

Despite the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), and accessibility services available at clothing stores, there are still many issues wheelchair users face when purchasing clothing in stores.

Accessible fitting rooms can be particularly frustrating for wheelchair users. Often they are left messy or used for stock and storage. It's also common for users without disabilities to use these rooms, forcing wheelchair users to wait in line.

How can we improve the clothing purchase experience for wheelchair users?

Our Process

Design Outcomes
Reserve a Fitting Room Online

Users can reserve fitting rooms in advance to reduce the waiting time. And they can block enough time as needed to try on clothes so donâ€™t have to worry about someone waiting in line outside.

Pre-ordering Clothes to Try-On

Pre-ordering clothes online and in-store to solve the problem that wheelchair users have trouble carrying items using wheelchairs while navigating through stores, also the issue with reachability to the clothes.

Scan to Check Fittingness

Before users head to the fitting room and try on the clothing, they can know about the fit of the clothes, reducing the unnecessary time and effort to try on clothes.

Research

We started the project by understanding wheelchair users' current experiences of clothing shopping at physical stores. To answer the following questions:

  • What issues do the users face?
  • What do they do to encounter this issue?
  • What is their motivation to purchase clothes at physical outlets?

Survey

We launched an online survey with questions about current shopping behaviors, experiences, and opinions. We distributed our survey online through forums, Facebook groups, Craigslist, and Reddit. We received 27 respondents.

Wheelchair users need the opportunity to visit physical stores.
  • Wheelchair users have the same need as other people to see, touch, and feel products as well as take items home immediately.
  • In-store shopping is a unique experience that has influenced their lives in different ways. For them, in-store shopping is also a chance to get out of the room and have a good time with their friends or families.
  • They are very opinionated on their appearance. A good number of people are stylish in their choice of clothes.

Interview

We Interviewed 4 participants including two wheelchair users, an occupational therapist, and a research scientist who specializes inaccessibility.

Key User Insights

From our survey and interviews, we created an affinity map as a group tp identify three major problems wheelchair users face when shopping in a physical store.

Persona

To better guide our design and enable everyone on the team to empathize with our users, I came up with the following personas:

Our key users are adults across a wide range of ages who are not able to stand or walk, and who use wheelchairs in their daily lives. They are strong-willed, independent, and go about doing their daily tasks, activities, and responsibilities.

User Feedback

After we finished the paper sketch and storyboards for our design concepts, we launched a survey and interviewed experts to gather user feedback. We got many valuable insights and reconsidered on how we could better design our final solution.

1. Privacy Concerns

The idea to have a body scanner was dropped with concerns over privacy. Since the owner organizations of the devices would have access to users' body measurements, a lot of responses from our survey had highlight privacy concerns. And our users would not like having been captured through a camera in a fitting room.

2. Accuracy Limitations

Another challenge of a body scanner was the accuracy of the device. The technology already exists, but it is not accurate enough. Considering the number of such devices that are needed to be stationed at the store and their cost, this idea could be taken into account in another 5-10 years.

Design Alternatives

Instead of a body scanner, we ask users to enter their body measurements through the application, so the data could stay in users' phones. And we will evaluate the fittingness based on the body measurements, which will be more accurate.

Evaluation

5 users helped us perform usability testing. As they performed tasks, we asked them to think aloud. Once the users completed their tasks, we asked several follow-up questions. Then they rated our design through the questionnaire based on SUS. The SUS result is 84.375, indicating that users are very satisfied.

Future Improvements

  • Use animations & GIFs as the measurement instruction images instead of static ones, so users would more clearly understand how to measure their bodies.
  • There should be a measurement service set up in-store offering assistance and tools for users to measure their bodies
  • Adjust timeslots for reserving fitting rooms, we need to research about average time that users spend in fitting rooms.
  • Add instructions to familiarize novice users with our app, using onboarding illustrations to educate users on how our app works.
